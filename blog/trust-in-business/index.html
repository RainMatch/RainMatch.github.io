In Business, the Most Important Product Is Your Name

Why trust is the foundation of every successful deal

Have Customers Trust YOU

Early in my career in sales and business development, I developed a simple personal philosophy that has guided every deal I've ever worked on:

"Have customers trust YOU."

I emphasize YOU because trust is rarely about the product first. It's about the person behind it.

When customers see you as a trustworthy person, something powerful happens. They will take your call. They will agree to meet. They will listen to what you have to say — no matter what product or service you are offering at the moment.

But if that trust doesn't exist, even the best product in the world struggles to get attention.

Over the years, I started thinking about my own name as the real brand I'm selling. Products change. Companies change. Markets change. AI is everywhere. But trust built with people — not through technology — compounds over time.

The most valuable asset in business is the relationship built on trust.

Research on Trust in Business

Several studies reinforce how central trust is to business relationships and purchasing decisions.

The Edelman Trust Barometer consistently shows that trust is one of the most important factors in business relationships. One of its key findings:

81%

of consumers say trust is a deciding factor in purchasing decisions.

Source: Edelman Trust Barometer

An article published by Harvard Business Review explains how trust affects collaboration and decision-making. The research highlights that high-trust environments lead to:

  • Higher engagement
  • Stronger collaboration
  • Better business outcomes

Research from LinkedIn shows that trusted recommendations strongly influence B2B purchasing decisions, especially in enterprise environments where relationships play a major role.

"Even as AI continues to transform how buyers and sellers interact, trust and relationships will remain at the heart of B2B decision-making."

"Being heavily recommended by customers and colleagues proved to be more than 3X as influential at this stage than a product that promised to be better — i.e. have more features, benefits, dimensions. These same customer and colleague recommendations were also 3X more influential than products that were more competitive on price."

These studies reinforce something that many experienced sales professionals already know intuitively: trust is the true currency of business relationships.

People often think business decisions are purely rational — based on features, pricing, or technical specifications. But in reality, most decisions involve risk. When companies buy a product or service, they are trusting the people behind it.

That's why trusted relationships dramatically accelerate deals.

When a customer trusts you, conversations start from a very different place. Instead of skepticism, there is openness. Instead of trying to prove credibility from scratch, credibility is already assumed.

This is also why warm introductions are so powerful in business. Trust transfers through relationships. When someone trusted introduces you, a large part of the credibility gap is already closed.

In many industries, especially complex B2B markets, deals happen because someone says:

"You should talk to this person."

Practical Tactics for Building Trust

Here are three simple but powerful practices that consistently help build trust in business relationships.

1 Protect Your Reputation Relentlessly

Your reputation is a long-term asset. Never recommend something you don't genuinely believe in. Never make promises you can't keep. And if something goes wrong, take responsibility quickly and transparently.

Trust compounds over time, but it can also disappear quickly. Protect it carefully.

2 Focus on Long-Term Relationships, Not Single Deals

Many people approach networking as a transactional activity. But the most successful professionals think differently. They invest in relationships long before any deal exists. They help others without expecting immediate returns. Over time, these relationships naturally lead to opportunities.

The strongest business networks are built on mutual trust and shared success.
